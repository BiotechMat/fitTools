## What it is

ZMA is a branded combination of **zinc, magnesium aspartate and vitamin B6**,
sold as a night-time recovery and "testosterone support" supplement. It's really
just two minerals and a vitamin in one capsule.

## What the evidence shows

**Testosterone and strength.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
This is the headline claim, and it doesn't hold up. In people who **aren't
deficient** in zinc or magnesium — most people who train and eat reasonably —
controlled studies do not show meaningful increases in testosterone or strength.

**Recovery and sleep.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Any benefit here traces back to correcting a genuine zinc or magnesium shortfall
(see [magnesium](/supplements/magnesium)), not to ZMA as a special formula.

**Correcting a real deficiency.** <EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" />
Zinc and magnesium do matter, and fixing a true deficiency has value — but that's
an argument for the individual minerals where needed, not for ZMA's marketing.

## Who it suits

Realistically, few people specifically need ZMA. If you're low in zinc or
magnesium, addressing that directly is more sensible than buying the branded
combination for its testosterone promise.

## Practical notes

You can get the same nutrients from food or plain zinc and magnesium. If you try
it, the muscle-building marketing is the part to ignore — judge it only on
whether you had a deficiency worth correcting.
