## What it is

L-carnitine helps shuttle fatty acids into cells to be burned for energy. That
real biological role is the basis for its long-standing reputation as a
"fat-burner", a reputation that runs well ahead of the evidence.

## What the evidence shows

**Fat loss.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" basis="human" />
Its role in fat metabolism doesn't translate into meaningful weight loss from a
supplement in most people. As a weight-loss aid the evidence is weak.

**Recovery and blood flow.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
The more interesting, still-preliminary data is around exercise recovery, with
some studies reporting reduced markers of muscle damage rather than fat loss.

## Who it suits

There is little compelling case for most healthy, meat-eating people, who
already have plenty of carnitine and make their own. Any potential upside is in
recovery, not fat loss.

## Practical notes

Because deficiency is uncommon in people who eat meat, topping up rarely does
much. If fat loss is the goal, an energy deficit, adequate protein and training
do the heavy lifting, a supplement can't substitute for those.
