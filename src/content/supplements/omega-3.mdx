## What it is

Omega-3s are essential fats, the important ones being EPA and DHA from oily fish
(or algae). Most people who rarely eat oily fish get relatively little, which is
the main rationale for supplementing.

## What the evidence shows

**Recovery and muscle.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
There's early, promising evidence that EPA/DHA may modestly support muscle
protein synthesis and reduce muscle soreness. It's preliminary, a reasonable
"might help a little", not a proven training aid.

**General health rationale.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Omega-3s have a broader, better-studied role in health than in fitness. That's a
legitimate reason to make sure you get enough, separate from any gym benefit.

**Fat loss and big performance claims.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
Not supported as a meaningful, standalone effect.

## Who it suits

People who seldom eat oily fish and want to cover their EPA/DHA intake. If you
eat fish a couple of times a week, you may not need a supplement at all.

## Practical notes

What counts is the **EPA + DHA** content, not the total oil weight, check the
label, since many cheap products are low in both. Freshness matters (rancid oil
is unpleasant and pointless), and **algae-based** omega-3 is a good vegan option.
