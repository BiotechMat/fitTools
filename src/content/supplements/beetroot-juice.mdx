## What it is

Beetroot juice is a natural source of dietary nitrate. Your body converts that
nitrate to nitrite and then to nitric oxide, a molecule that relaxes blood
vessels and improves how efficiently working muscles use oxygen. The active
ingredient is the nitrate, not the beetroot itself — which is why concentrated
"shots" standardised for nitrate are used in research rather than a splash of
beetroot in a blend.

## What the evidence shows

**Endurance and exercise economy.** <EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" />
Dietary nitrate is one of the better-evidenced endurance aids. Across human
trials it can modestly improve exercise economy and time to exhaustion,
especially in recreational athletes. Highly-trained athletes tend to respond
less, and individual response varies a lot.

**Short, high-intensity and team-sport efforts.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
There is earlier evidence for benefits to repeated sprints and high-intensity
efforts, but it is less consistent than the endurance data.

## Who it suits

Endurance and recreational athletes chasing a small, legal edge — particularly
those who aren't already highly trained. It is a food-based option with a good
safety record for most people.

## Practical notes

Studies usually dose it a couple of hours before exercise, since blood nitrite
peaks with a delay. Avoid antibacterial mouthwash around that window — it kills
the mouth bacteria needed to activate the nitrate. Harmless red or pink urine
is normal. Test it in training before relying on it, and pair it with sensible
pacing using our [heart-rate zone calculator](/heart-rate-zone-calculator).
