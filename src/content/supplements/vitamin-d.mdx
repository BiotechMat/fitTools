## What it is

Vitamin D is a fat-soluble vitamin your skin makes from sunlight and you get in
small amounts from food. It's essential for bone health, immune function and
muscle, and deficiency is common — especially in winter at northern latitudes.

## What the evidence shows

**Correcting a deficiency.** <EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" />
That low vitamin D is bad for bone and general health, and that fixing a genuine
deficiency is worthwhile, is well established. This is the real reason to care
about vitamin D.

**A performance or strength boost when you're already replete.**
<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> Most apparent "performance"
benefits come from correcting a deficiency. Topping up someone whose levels are
already adequate has not been shown to reliably improve strength or endurance.

**Testosterone and body-composition claims.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
Marketed hard, but the effect (where any) again traces back to correcting a
deficiency, not to supplementing replete people.

## Who it suits

People at real risk of deficiency — little sun exposure, darker skin, covering
up, northern winters — ideally guided by a blood test. If you're replete,
there's little to gain.

## Practical notes

**Test, don't guess.** A simple blood test tells you whether you actually need
it, and D3 (cholecalciferol) raises levels more effectively than D2. Read the
safety box — this is a vitamin where far more is genuinely worse, not better.
