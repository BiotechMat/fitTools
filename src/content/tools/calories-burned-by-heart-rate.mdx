## Methodology

The estimate uses the Keytel et al. (2005) equations, developed on 115
regularly-exercising adults during steady cycling and treadmill work
against measured oxygen consumption:

- Men: `kJ/min = −55.0969 + 0.6309 × HR + 0.1988 × weight (kg) +
  0.2017 × age`
- Women: `kJ/min = −20.4022 + 0.4472 × HR − 0.1263 × weight (kg) +
  0.074 × age`

Kilojoules convert to kilocalories at 4.184, and the per-minute rate is
multiplied by your session duration. These are the equations (without
the VO2max term) that much of the consumer heart-rate industry builds
on.

## Interpreting your result

The relationship between heart rate and energy expenditure is close to
linear during steady aerobic exercise, that's the model's home ground.
Use your session-average heart rate from a strap or watch, and treat
the output as a consistent estimate for comparing your own sessions
rather than a measurement.

Fitness shifts the line: a fitter person burns more at the same heart
rate than the population average predicts (the published model with a
VO2max term corrects for this; the version here deliberately needs no
lab value).

## Limitations

- Valid for steady aerobic work at roughly 90 bpm upwards, the tool
  warns rather than reporting numbers below that.
- Interval sessions blur the average; the estimate degrades as work
  becomes less steady.
- Resistance training raises heart rate out of proportion to oxygen
  use, so heart-rate estimates overshoot badly there, use the
  MET-based calculator instead.
- Stress, heat, dehydration and caffeine all raise heart rate without
  raising energy expenditure.
