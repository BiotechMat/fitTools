## Methodology

Three published field methods, implemented exactly as their papers
specify:

- **Cooper 12-minute run** (JAMA, 1968): VO2max = (distance in metres
  − 504.9) ÷ 44.73. Cooper validated the all-out 12-minute run against
  treadmill-measured oxygen uptake in Air Force personnel, with a
  correlation of about 0.90.
- **Rockport 1-mile walk** (Kline et al., 1987): a regression on weight,
  age, sex, walk time and finish heart rate, developed on 343 adults
  aged 30 to 69. We convert your weight to pounds internally, inputs stay
  metric.
- **Heart-rate ratio** (Uth et al., 2004): VO2max ≈ 15.3 × (HRmax ÷
  HRrest), derived from the Fick principle and validated in trained
  men. No exercise test required.

## Interpreting your result

VO2max is reported relative to body weight (ml/kg/min), so it improves
both by getting aerobically fitter and by carrying less non-functional
mass. Healthy untrained adults typically sit in the mid-30s to mid-40s;
endurance-trained athletes commonly exceed 60.

The three methods will not agree exactly, each carries its own error,
and the walk test saturates for fit people who can't get their heart
rate up by walking. Pick the method that matches how you can actually
test, and re-test the same way.

## Limitations

- Field estimates carry meaningful individual error (several ml/kg/min);
  laboratory testing remains the gold standard.
- The Cooper equation assumes an honest, evenly-paced maximal effort,
  pacing errors dominate the result.
- The heart-rate ratio method needs a true measured maximum heart rate;
  an age-predicted guess propagates its own error straight into the
  estimate.
- The Rockport regression was developed on 30 to 69-year-olds walking,
  outside that population it extrapolates.
