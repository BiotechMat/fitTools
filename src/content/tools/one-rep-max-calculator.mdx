## Methodology

The calculator estimates a one-repetition maximum from a submaximal set
using four published equations, where `w` is the weight lifted and `r` the
reps completed:

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr><th scope="col">Formula</th><th scope="col">Estimated 1RM</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Epley (default)</td><td><code>w × (1 + r ÷ 30)</code></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Brzycki</td><td><code>w × 36 ÷ (37 − r)</code></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Lombardi</td><td><code>w × r^0.10</code></td></tr>
    <tr><td>O'Conner</td><td><code>w × (1 + 0.025 × r)</code></td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

Estimates are treated as valid for sets of **ten reps or fewer** — beyond
that, the relationship between reps and maximal strength degrades and the
tool warns you. The working-weight table converts your estimated max into
loads at 50–95% in five-point steps, the percentages most programmes
prescribe.

## Interpreting your result

Prediction accuracy is genuinely good in the useful range: comparisons
against tested maxima (LeSuer et al., 1997) found correlations above 0.95,
with typical errors of a few percent for bench press and squat. Expect the
formulas to agree closely for sets of 1–5 reps and drift apart as reps
climb — at ten reps Epley and Brzycki converge, while Lombardi and
O'Conner run lower.

The estimate's best use is programming: percentage-based training works
perfectly well off an estimated max, without the fatigue and injury risk
of testing a true single.

## Limitations

- Rep-to-max relationships are individual: fibre type, training history
  and technique all shift where you fall against any formula.
- Accuracy varies by lift — published comparisons found deadlift maxima
  consistently underestimated, so calibrate per exercise.
- The set you enter must be honestly close to failure; a comfortable
  five-rep set predicts nothing about your maximum.
- An estimated 1RM is not a licence to attempt one. Maximal singles carry
  their own risk and belong in a structured programme with good technique.
