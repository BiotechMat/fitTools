## Methodology

Caffeine elimination follows first-order kinetics — a fixed *proportion*
is cleared per unit time — so remaining caffeine is:

`C(t) = dose × 0.5^(t ÷ t½)`

The default half-life of 5 hours reflects the commonly cited average for
healthy adults (Institute of Medicine review); the slider spans 1.5–9.5
hours because real half-lives vary that widely with genetics (notably
CYP1A2 variants), smoking, pregnancy and medications such as some oral
contraceptives. The chart plots the decay over 24 hours against your
chosen sleep threshold, and the headline figure solves for when the
curve crosses it.

## Interpreting your result

The practical question is bedtime interference. A 200 mg mid-afternoon
coffee still leaves ~50 mg circulating at a 5-hour half-life ten hours
later — enough to measurably degrade sleep quality in sensitive people
even when it doesn't stop you falling asleep. Work backwards: pick the
threshold below which you sleep well (many people land somewhere near
50 mg), and the calculator tells you your effective caffeine curfew.

EFSA's safety assessment puts habitual intakes up to ~400 mg/day within
safe limits for healthy adults — the issue this tool addresses is
timing, not toxicity.

## Limitations

- You rarely know your own half-life; if caffeine obviously disturbs
  your sleep, assume you sit at the slow end and set the slider high.
- Doses stack: the model shows a single dose, so a second coffee adds
  its own curve on top.
- Sensitivity to a given blood level also varies — the threshold is
  personal and found by experiment.
