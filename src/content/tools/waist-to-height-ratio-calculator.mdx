## Methodology

The arithmetic could not be simpler, waist circumference divided by
height, both in centimetres. The evidence sits in the boundary: Browning,
Hsieh and Ashwell's systematic review of 78 studies across fourteen
countries found that a ratio of **0.5** worked as a global screening
boundary for cardiovascular and diabetes risk, in men and women and
across ethnic groups. Hence the public-health phrasing the review
popularised: *keep your waist to less than half your height*.

The bands shown at 0.4 and 0.6 are the conventional chart edges around
that boundary, below 0.4 is unusually lean; 0.5 to 0.6 flags increased
risk worth acting on; 0.6 and above flags substantially higher risk.

## Interpreting your result

Central fat, the fat this ratio detects, is more metabolically harmful
than fat carried elsewhere, which is why a simple tape measurement often
screens cardiometabolic risk better than BMI. The two tools disagree most
usefully in muscular people (high BMI, healthy ratio) and in
normal-weight people with abdominal fat (healthy BMI, raised ratio).

Measure at the midpoint between your lowest rib and the top of the hip
bone, after a normal breath out, tape snug but not compressing.

## Limitations

- A screening measure, not a diagnosis, a raised ratio is a prompt to
  discuss risk with a professional, not a verdict.
- Measurement technique moves the number: same landmarks, same time of
  day, every time.
- The 0.5 boundary is a population screening value; individual risk
  depends on far more than one ratio.
