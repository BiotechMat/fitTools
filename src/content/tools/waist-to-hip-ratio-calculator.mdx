## Methodology

Waist circumference divided by hip circumference. The cut-offs come from
the WHO expert consultation on waist circumference and waist–hip ratio
(2008): a ratio of **0.90 or above in men** and **0.85 or above in
women** marks substantially increased risk of metabolic complications.

Measurement protocol matters and the WHO report specifies it: waist at
the midpoint between the lowest rib and the iliac crest, hips at the
widest portion of the buttocks, tape parallel to the floor, at the end
of a normal expiration.

## Interpreting your result

The ratio is a proxy for fat *distribution*. Two people at identical
weight and BMI can carry very different visceral fat, and it's the
abdominal depot that tracks most strongly with cardiovascular disease
and type 2 diabetes. A ratio under the cut-off doesn't guarantee low
risk — it says fat distribution isn't adding to it.

Waist-to-hip responds slowly to change, because both numbers move
together as you lose or gain; the raw waist measurement often shows
progress sooner.

## Limitations

- Screening, not diagnosis; the cut-offs are population values.
- Two measurement sites means twice the technique error — consistency
  of landmarks matters more than the tape's precision.
- After significant weight loss the hip measurement can fall faster
  than the waist, nudging the ratio the "wrong" way while health
  improves — track it alongside waist and weight, not alone.
