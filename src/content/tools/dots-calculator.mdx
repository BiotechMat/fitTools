## Methodology

All three scoring systems share one idea: multiply your total by a
coefficient derived from bodyweight so different-sized lifters can be
compared.

- **DOTS**, `total × 500 ÷ poly4(bodyweight)`, a fourth-degree
  polynomial fitted separately for men and women.
- **Wilks** (original), the same structure with a fifth-degree
  polynomial; the historical standard.
- **IPF GL points**, `total × 100 ÷ (A − B·e^(−C·bw))`, with parameter
  sets per sex, event (full power or bench-only) and equipment (raw or
  equipped); the system used at IPF competitions.

Every coefficient is taken verbatim from the OpenPowerlifting codebase,
the open-source project whose rankings the powerlifting world treats as
canonical, and our DOTS implementation is regression-tested against
OPL's own computed scores for eleven real meet entries.

## Interpreting your result

As rough waypoints for raw full-power DOTS: 200 to 300 covers most
recreational lifters, 300 to 450 is competitive club-to-national territory,
450 to 550 is national-to-international, and 600+ belongs to the sport's
outliers. Wilks runs on a similar scale; IPF GL centres differently
(100 GL ≈ a notional reference performance), so compare GL only with GL.

Scores are for comparing lifters at a moment in time, for tracking your
own progress, your total in kilograms is the honest metric, since your
score also moves with bodyweight.

## Limitations

- Formulas are fitted to competition populations; extremes of bodyweight
  sit on the least-supported parts of the curves (inputs are clamped to
  each formula's published range).
- DOTS and Wilks here assume a full-power total; only IPF GL has
  bench-only parameters.
- No formula fully removes the bodyweight debate. They are the accepted
  compromise, not a solved problem.
