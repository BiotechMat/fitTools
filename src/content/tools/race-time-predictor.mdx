## Methodology

Predictions use Peter Riegel's endurance formula, published in *American
Scientist* in 1981:

`T₂ = T₁ × (D₂ ÷ D₁)^1.06`

Riegel fitted the 1.06 exponent to record performances across running,
swimming and other endurance sports, capturing how sustainable pace
declines as distance grows. Half a century on, it remains the standard
first-order race predictor because it keeps working for trained runners
between adjacent distances.

## Interpreting your result

Read predictions as "what your engine could deliver, appropriately
trained for that distance". From a fresh 10k, the half-marathon
prediction is usually realistic if you're running the weekly mileage a
half demands; the marathon prediction assumes marathon training you may
not have done. Empirical studies of recreational runners (Vickers &
Vertosick, 2016) found actual marathon times average several minutes
slower than Riegel predicts, for exactly this reason.

The best use is target-setting: predict from a recent race, train
specifically for the target distance, and treat the prediction as your
optimistic goal pace.

## Limitations

- Accuracy degrades with distance gaps — predicting a marathon from a 5k
  compounds every assumption.
- The formula knows nothing about heat, hills, fuelling or race-day
  execution, which dominate marathon outcomes.
- Both directions matter: predicting downwards (marathon → 5k) assumes
  speed work you may equally not have done.
