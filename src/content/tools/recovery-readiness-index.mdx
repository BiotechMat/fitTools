## Methodology

The Recovery Readiness Index is an **open composite index** (v1.0.0), a
transparent, self-relative version of the daily "readiness" scores built
into wearables. Every input compares **today's value against your own
baseline**; there is no cross-user comparison and no clinical meaning.

Each metric becomes a 0 to 100 sub-score based on how far today deviates from
your usual figure, in the favourable or unfavourable direction, then
combined by weight:

  <thead>
    <tr><th scope="col">Input</th><th scope="col">Weight</th><th scope="col">Direction</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>HRV vs baseline</td><td>30%</td><td>higher is better</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Resting HR vs baseline</td><td>25%</td><td>lower is better</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Sleep vs your need</td><td>25%</td><td>meeting need is better</td></tr>
    <tr><td>Breathing rate vs baseline</td><td>20%</td><td>lower is better</td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

All four inputs are T2 (moderate/surrogate evidence). **A note on the
weights:** the published methodology proposed HRV at 40%, but our house rule
caps any single input at ~30%, so we adjusted the weighting (HRV 30, resting
HR 25, sleep 25, breathing 20). That change is recorded in the public
[index methodology changelog](/learn/index-methodology).

The sub-scores use a documented deviation mapping rather than a strict
z-score, because most people don't know their baseline's standard
deviation. You enter today's value and your usual value, and the score
reflects the proportional change.

## Interpreting your result

This is a **daily, self-relative** number. It only means something relative
to *you*. A score of 60 today versus 40 yesterday says your body's signals
are more favourable than yesterday, nothing about anyone else. Read the
breakdown to see which signal moved: suppressed HRV and an elevated resting
heart rate often show up together after poor sleep, alcohol, illness or hard
training.

Use it as one input alongside how you actually feel and what your week looks
like, not as an instruction to train or rest. The signals it uses are
genuinely informative for monitoring, but they're noisy day to day.

## Limitations

- **Self-relative only**, no clinical meaning, no cross-user comparison,
  not a medical test.
- Single-day readings are noisy; trends over several days are more
  trustworthy than any one score.
- Baselines matter: garbage-in figures give a misleading score. Use stable
  7 to 30 day averages from a consistent measurement method.
- HRV in particular depends heavily on how and when it's measured
  (overnight vs morning, device, position).
- It doesn't know *why* a signal changed, illness, stress, alcohol and
  travel all look similar to it.
