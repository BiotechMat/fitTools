## Methodology

The fat-free mass index normalises muscle mass to height:

`FFMI = fat-free mass (kg) ÷ height (m)²`, with
`fat-free mass = weight × (1 − body fat ÷ 100)`

Because raw FFMI drifts slightly with height, we also show the
**height-adjusted FFMI** from the original paper:
`adjusted = FFMI + 6.1 × (1.8 − height in m)`.

The metric comes from Kouri and colleagues' 1995 study comparing steroid
users and non-users: among 157 athletes, drug-free FFMI clustered below
~25 while users commonly exceeded it.

## Interpreting your result

As rough bands for men: 17 to 20 spans typical untrained to recreationally
trained physiques, 20 to 23 reflects serious training, and 23 to 25 approaches
the drug-free ceiling observed in the research. Women average roughly
2.5 to 3 points lower. FFMI is best used to track your own trajectory,
gaining a point of FFMI at stable body fat is a meaningful year's work for
an experienced lifter.

## Limitations

- Your FFMI is only as good as your body-fat estimate: ±3 percentage
  points of body-fat error moves FFMI by about ±1 point.
- The 25 "limit" is a statistical observation from one cohort, not a law
  of biology, genetic outliers exist.
- FFMI says nothing about strength, performance or health; it is a
  body-composition description only.
