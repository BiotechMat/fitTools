## Methodology

The calculator counts backwards from your alarm:

`bedtime = wake time − (cycles × 90 minutes) − 15 minutes to fall asleep`

Sleep is organised in cycles that pass through light sleep, deep
slow-wave sleep and REM. A full cycle averages roughly 90 minutes in
adults, the physiology literature puts the true range at about 70 to 120
minutes, lengthening as the night goes on. The 15-minute allowance
reflects typical sleep latency: the tool aims to start your *first full
cycle* when you are actually asleep, not when your head hits the pillow.

Options are shown for six cycles (9 hours of sleep), five (7.5 hours) and
four (6 hours). Five cycles lands inside the 7 to 9 hours the National Sleep
Foundation's expert panel recommends for most adults (Hirshkowitz et al.,
2015); four is a compromise for occasional short nights, not a target.

## Interpreting your result

The idea behind cycle-based bedtimes is to avoid the alarm landing in deep
sleep, when waking feels most groggy, that heavy-headed state is called
sleep inertia. Waking near a cycle boundary, when sleep is lightest, tends
to feel easier even at the same total duration.

Use the suggested times as starting points and run a simple experiment:
hold one bedtime for a week and note how waking feels. Consistency is the
real lever. A regular schedule does more for sleep quality than
precision-timed bedtimes ever will.

## Limitations

- Ninety minutes is an average. Your cycles may run 75 minutes or 110, and
  they lengthen across the night, so no calculator can time your cycles
  exactly.
- Sleep latency varies day to day with caffeine, stress, light and
  exercise timing; the fixed 15-minute allowance is a convenience.
- Alcohol, shift work and jet lag restructure sleep architecture entirely.
- Persistent difficulty falling asleep, staying asleep, or heavy daytime
  sleepiness warrants a conversation with your GP rather than a schedule
  tweak.
