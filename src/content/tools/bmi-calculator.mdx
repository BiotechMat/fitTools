## Methodology

Body mass index is weight divided by height squared:

`BMI = weight (kg) ÷ height (m)²`

Results are classed against the World Health Organization's adult
categories:

  </thead>

## Interpreting your result

BMI is best understood as a population screening measure: across large
groups it correlates with body fat and with health risk, which is why
health services still use it. For an individual, it is a starting point for
a conversation, not a conclusion. It knows nothing about your body
composition, training history, frame or ethnicity.

**The athlete problem deserves emphasis**: because muscle is denser than
fat, muscular people routinely register as overweight, many elite rugby
players and sprinters are technically obese by BMI. If you train
seriously, a body-fat estimate (see our US Navy calculator) tells you far
more than BMI can.

## Limitations

- BMI cannot distinguish fat from muscle, nor say where fat is carried,
  and central (abdominal) fat carries most of the metabolic risk.
- The WHO notes that risk thresholds sit lower for some Asian populations;
  a single set of global cut-offs fits nobody perfectly.
- Adult cut-offs do not apply to children, adolescents or pregnancy.
- At the individual level, waist circumference, body-fat percentage and
  clinical markers measured with your GP are all more informative than a
  height-to-weight ratio from 19th-century statistics.
