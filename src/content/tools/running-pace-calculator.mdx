## Methodology

Pure arithmetic, done carefully:

`pace = time ÷ distance` — displayed per kilometre and per mile
(1 mile = 1609.344 m exactly), with speed as its reciprocal and
cumulative kilometre splits assuming perfectly even pacing.

Nothing is estimated or modelled here; the only assumptions are the ones
you type in.

## Interpreting your result

Pace is the runner's working currency: training plans prescribe it, races
are paced by it, and progress shows up in it. The splits table is the
practical output — write the key ones on your hand or set them on your
watch, and you know at every kilometre marker whether you're on plan.

Even pacing is the strategy to beat: analyses of distance-running records
consistently show near-even or slightly negative splits (second half
marginally faster) produce the best times. If your target pace only feels
sustainable with a fast start, the target — not the pacing — is the
problem.

## Limitations

- GPS watches routinely measure road races long by 0.5–1%, so your
  watch pace will read slightly slower than your official pace — the
  official distance is what counts.
- Hills, wind and heat change the effort a pace costs; effort-based
  running (or heart-rate zones) handles rough conditions better than
  rigid splits.
- Track distances are exact; trail-race distances often aren't. Adjust
  expectations accordingly.
