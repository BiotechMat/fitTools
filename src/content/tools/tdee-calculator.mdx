## Methodology

This calculator estimates your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) in two
steps: it first estimates your basal metabolic rate (BMR), then multiplies it
by an activity factor.

By default, BMR is estimated with the **Mifflin-St Jeor equation**, published
in the *American Journal of Clinical Nutrition* in 1990 and repeatedly found
to be the most reliable of the common prediction equations for healthy
adults:

  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Men</td><td><code>BMR = 10 × weight (kg) + 6.25 × height (cm) − 5 × age (years) + 5</code></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Women</td><td><code>BMR = 10 × weight (kg) + 6.25 × height (cm) − 5 × age (years) − 161</code></td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

This is the simplified form proposed for practice in the original paper; the
paper&rsquo;s full regression coefficients (9.99 for weight and 4.92 for age)
round to the values above.

If you know your body-fat percentage from a reliable measurement, you can
switch to the **Katch-McArdle equation**, which works from fat-free mass
(FFM) instead of total weight:

`BMR = 370 + 21.6 × FFM (kg)`, where `FFM = weight × (1 − body fat ÷ 100)`

The **revised Harris-Benedict equation** (Roza &amp; Shizgal, 1984) is also
available as a toggle for comparison. It is an older model that tends to
estimate slightly higher than Mifflin-St Jeor for most people:

  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Men</td><td><code>BMR = 88.362 + 13.397 × weight + 4.799 × height − 5.677 × age</code></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Women</td><td><code>BMR = 447.593 + 9.247 × weight + 3.098 × height − 4.330 × age</code></td></tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

Your BMR is then multiplied by a standard activity factor, 1.2 (sedentary),
1.375 (lightly active), 1.55 (moderately active), 1.725 (very active) or 1.9
(extremely active), to estimate the total calories you burn in a normal day.

## Interpreting your result

Your TDEE is an estimate of **maintenance calories**: eat around this amount
and your weight should stay roughly stable. A sustained intake below it tends
to produce weight loss; a sustained intake above it tends to produce weight
gain. Because every figure here is an estimate, the most useful next step is
to track your weight for two to four weeks and adjust intake based on what
actually happens, rather than treating the number as exact.

## Limitations

- Prediction equations describe the average person of your size, age and
  sex. Individual BMR commonly varies by up to about 10% either side of the
  prediction, and more in people with very high or very low muscle mass.
- Activity factors are the biggest source of error. Most people overestimate
  their activity level; if in doubt, pick the lower option.
- Mifflin-St Jeor was derived in healthy, mostly non-athletic adults. For
  very muscular people, Katch-McArdle with a *measured* body-fat percentage
  is usually the better choice.
- Energy expenditure adapts as you diet (adaptive thermogenesis), so a
  deficit that works initially will slowly shrink; recalculate as your
  weight changes.
