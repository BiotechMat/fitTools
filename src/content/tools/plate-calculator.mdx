## Methodology

The calculator solves a small but genuinely fiddly gym problem: which
plates go on each side of the bar?

`per-side load = (target weight − bar weight) ÷ 2`

The tool then builds that per-side load from your available plates,
heaviest first — the conventional way to load a bar. Where a simple
heaviest-first pass would miss a valid combination (sparse racks create
these cases: loading 30 kg per side from only 20s and 15s requires passing
over the 20), the tool searches every combination your inventory allows,
so if a load *can* be built, it will be found.

When your exact target is impossible with the plates available, the
calculator returns the **nearest achievable weight**, above or below, with
ties resolved to the lighter load. The default inventory follows standard
metric plate denominations (25 / 20 / 15 / 10 / 5 / 2.5 / 1.25 kg, as in
the IPF technical rules), and any change you make to it is remembered on
this device.

## Interpreting your result

The headline is the per-side stack, listed in the order you'd slide the
plates on. "Total" is what the bar will actually weigh — check it against
your programme when the tool reports a nearest-achievable load rather than
an exact one.

If your gym's smallest pair is 2.5 kg plates, your minimum jump is 5 kg on
the bar — often too coarse for pressing progress. A pair of 1.25 kg
(or microplates) is the cheapest training upgrade there is.

## Limitations

- Inventory counts are per side: two 25s per side means four 25 kg plates
  in the gym. Count what's actually free when the gym is busy.
- Bar weights vary — worn or non-standard bars can be a kilogram or more
  off their nominal weight, and collars (typically 2.5 kg each in
  competition) are not included here.
- Imperial plate sets aren't yet supported; kilogram racks only for now.
