## Methodology

The baseline comes from the European Food Safety Authority's dietary
reference values: adequate intakes of **2.0 L/day for women and 2.5 L/day
for men of total water** under moderate conditions. "Total" matters —
food typically supplies 20–30% of it, so the drinking target is lower
than the headline figure and the results panel shows both.

For exercise, the calculator adds your chosen sweat rate times training
duration. The 0.3–2.0 L/h slider range reflects the span reported in the
ACSM's position stand on exercise and fluid replacement (Sawka et al.,
2007); most people training moderately indoors sit near the lower half.

## Interpreting your result

Treat the output as a guideline centre-point, not a daily quota. EFSA's
values are adequate intakes — amounts that keep typical healthy people
well hydrated — rather than minimum requirements, and day-to-day needs
move with heat, activity, body size and diet. Pale-straw urine and rarely
feeling thirsty are better evidence of adequate hydration than any
counted litre.

## Limitations

- Individual needs vary widely; kidney conditions, heart failure,
  pregnancy and some medications change fluid requirements — follow
  medical advice over any calculator.
- Sweat rate is the biggest unknown; weighing yourself before and after a
  hard session (1 kg ≈ 1 L) beats guessing the slider position.
- Drinking far beyond losses risks hyponatraemia — more is not better.
