## Methodology

Three honest approximations chained together:

1. **Stride length** ≈ `0.415 × height` for men, `0.413 × height` for
   women — the standard pedometry estimating factors.
2. **Distance** = steps × stride length.
3. **Energy** via the walking MET for your chosen pace (Compendium codes
   17170/17190/17200), using `kcal/min = MET × 3.5 × kg ÷ 200` over the
   time the distance implies at that pace.

Each link adds error, which is why the page badges itself a rough
estimate — the arithmetic is exact, the assumptions are averages.

## Interpreting your result

Step-based calorie figures are most useful as a consistent daily signal
rather than precise accounting. If your tracker says 9,000 steps every
day and your weight is stable, that activity is already priced into your
maintenance calories; a genuinely extra 5,000-step walk is worth roughly
150–250 kcal for most people.

Remember these calories include resting metabolism for the walking time —
don't stack them on top of a TDEE that already reflects an active
lifestyle.

## Limitations

- Real stride length varies with pace, terrain, footwear and habit;
  counting your own steps over a measured 100 m beats the height factor.
- Phone and wrist trackers miscount steps in both directions.
- Walking uphill, on sand, or with load can double the energy cost — the
  walking METs assume firm, level ground.
