## Methodology

The calculator multiplies your body weight by a grams-per-kilogram band
taken directly from the research for each goal — the bands are the
findings, not our inventions:

- **General training — 1.4–2.0 g/kg**: the International Society of
  Sports Nutrition position stand's recommendation for building and
  maintaining muscle in exercising people.
- **Build muscle — 1.6–2.2 g/kg**: Morton and colleagues' meta-analysis
  of 49 resistance-training trials found gains plateau at roughly
  1.6 g/kg, with the upper 95% confidence bound at 2.2 g/kg — the band
  spans that uncertainty.
- **Lose fat — 1.8–2.0 g/kg**: in an energy deficit, higher protein
  helps preserve lean mass while fat is lost (Phillips & Van Loon).
- **65 and over — 1.2–1.5 g/kg**: the PROT-AGE expert group recommends
  at least 1.0–1.2 g/kg for older adults, and more for the active, to
  counter anabolic resistance and age-related muscle loss.

The per-meal figure applies Schoenfeld and Aragon's practical target of
~0.4 g/kg per meal across at least four meals; the RDA line (0.8 g/kg)
is shown for contrast as the sedentary minimum, not a training target.

## Interpreting your result

Anywhere inside your band works — the difference between the bottom and
top of a range matters far less than hitting it consistently. Heavier
individuals with high body fat can sensibly compute from a weight closer
to their goal weight rather than current weight, since lean tissue drives
the requirement.

Protein is the most satiating macronutrient, which is why cutting
protocols hold it high while calories fall.

## Limitations

- Bands are for healthy adults; kidney disease changes the calculus —
  follow your clinician.
- Requirements scale with lean mass, not fat mass, so g/kg of total
  weight over-estimates for very high body-fat levels.
- Elite endurance athletes in heavy training blocks sit at the top of
  the general band, sometimes above it — context the single number
  can't carry.
