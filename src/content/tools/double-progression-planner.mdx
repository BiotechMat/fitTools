## Methodology

Double progression advances two variables in strict sequence:

1. At a fixed load, add **reps** session to session within your range
   (say 6 to 8).
2. Only when **every set** reaches the top of the range, add the
   **increment** and reset reps to the bottom.

The planner encodes exactly that rule: enter last session's reps per set
and it prescribes next session. All sets at the top → load + increment ×
bottom of range; anything less → same load, chase the top.

## Interpreting your result

The system's strength is its patience. Between load jumps you accumulate
volume at a weight you can handle with good technique, and the jump only
arrives once you've demonstrated mastery, every set, not just the
first. This makes it well suited to dumbbell and machine work, where the
next increment is often a brutal 5 to 10% jump, and to accessory lifts where
grinding maximal singles makes no sense.

A "+" after the rep target means you're still in the rep-building phase.
Failing to add any reps across three or more sessions usually signals
recovery, sleep or nutrition problems rather than a programming one.

## Limitations

- Rep quality counts: reps ground out with degrading form postpone the
  problem to the next load. Count honest reps.
- Beginners on barbell compounds can usually progress load every session
  and don't need double progression yet.
- Research supports multiple progression schemes; this one is a robust
  default, not the only road.
