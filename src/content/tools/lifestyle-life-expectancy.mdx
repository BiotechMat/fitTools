## Methodology

This tool reports a finding from Li et al., *Circulation* 2018 — a large,
long-running study of US health professionals (the Nurses' Health Study and
the Health Professionals Follow-up Study). The researchers defined five
**low-risk lifestyle factors** and estimated life expectancy at age 50 by
how many of them people met:

- **Never smoked**
- **BMI 18.5–24.9**
- **≥ 30 minutes/day** of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity
- **Moderate alcohol** — 5–15 g/day (women), 5–30 g/day (men)
- **High diet quality** — top 40% on a validated diet score

At age 50, people meeting **all five** factors had a projected life
expectancy about **14.0 years longer (women)** and **12.2 years longer
(men)** than those meeting **none**. This tool counts the factors you meet
and shows those published figures.

**Why we don't show a number for 1–4 factors.** The paper published exact
projected figures only for the extremes (none and all five). It reported a
graded relationship — more factors, more years — but did not tabulate the
in-between values, so putting a precise number on "3 of 5" would be
inventing data the study never published. We won't do that.

## Interpreting your result — read this carefully

This is a **population-level association, not a personal prediction.** It
describes what happened, on average, across tens of thousands of people in
one cohort. It cannot tell *you* how long you will live — that depends on
genetics, chance, healthcare, and much more that no lifestyle count
captures.

It is also correlational. People who meet all five factors tend to differ
in many other ways (income, education, healthcare access) that also affect
lifespan, so not all of the difference is caused by the habits themselves.
The honest takeaway is encouraging but modest: these habits were linked to
meaningfully more years in this study, and they're worth doing for many
reasons — but the number is not a forecast for you.

## Limitations

- **Association, not causation**, and **not a prediction** for any
  individual.
- The cohort was mostly white US health professionals — it may not
  generalise to everyone.
- "Moderate alcohol" is a narrow band, and both zero and higher intake
  carried more risk than moderate in the data. Nothing here is a reason to
  start or increase drinking.
- Diet quality was measured with a specific validated score; a simple
  self-assessment is a rough proxy.
- This is educational, not medical advice.
