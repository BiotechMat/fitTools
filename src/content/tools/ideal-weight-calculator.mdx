## Methodology

The calculator reports all four classic ideal-body-weight formulas. Each
takes the same shape — a base weight plus an increment per inch of height
over five feet:

  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr><td>Devine (1974)</td><td><code>50.0 + 2.3/in</code></td><td><code>45.5 + 2.3/in</code></td></tr>
    <tr><td>Robinson (1983)</td><td><code>51.65 + 1.85/in</code></td><td><code>48.67 + 1.65/in</code></td></tr>

One point of care: for the Robinson formula we implement the coefficients
from the **original 1983 publication** (51.65 + 1.85 for men, 48.67 + 1.65
for women). Most calculators use a rounded variant (52 + 1.9, 49 + 1.7)
that circulated via later reviews, so our Robinson figures can differ from
other sites by up to about half a kilogram — ours match the source paper.

## Interpreting your result

These formulas were never health guidance. Devine proposed his equation in
1974 to standardise drug dosing; the others refined it against insurance
height–weight tables of their era. That the four "ideals" for the same
height disagree by several kilograms is the most honest thing about them —
there is no single correct weight for a height, and the range we report is
the point, not a target band to aim for.

## Limitations

- Height and sex are the only inputs: body composition, frame and age are
  invisible to every one of these formulas.
- All four are undefined below five feet, and behave increasingly oddly at
  very tall heights — artefacts of their linear form.
- The underlying reference data is 40–110 years old and predominantly
  reflects white, insured, mid-century populations.
- For actual health assessment, body-fat percentage, waist measurement and
  clinical markers with your GP supersede anything here (Pai & Paloucek,
  2000, is the definitive history of why).
