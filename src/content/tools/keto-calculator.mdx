## Methodology

The calculator is honest arithmetic on top of published pieces, with no
keto-specific magic:

1. **Energy**: resting metabolic rate from the Mifflin–St Jeor equation,
   multiplied by the standard activity factor, then adjusted for your
   goal (−20% to lose, +10% to gain) — the same engine as the TDEE
   calculator.
2. **Carbohydrate**: fixed in grams at your chosen cap. Ketogenic diets
   are conventionally defined by restricting to roughly 20–50 g of net
   carbs per day; the default sits at 25 g.
3. **Protein**: body weight × your chosen g/kg from the evidence-based
   1.6–2.2 range (Morton et al. 2018) — ketosis does not change protein
   requirements.
4. **Fat**: every kilocalorie left after carbs and protein, at
   9 kcal/g. Fat is the adjustable energy lever of the diet, not a
   target with independent meaning.

## Interpreting your result

The ISSN's review of diet types is worth internalising: across
approaches from low-fat to ketogenic, body-composition outcomes are
similar when calories and protein are matched. Choose keto because the
food pattern suits your adherence, hunger and preferences — the deficit
does the fat loss, whichever diet delivers it.

Expect a rapid early weight drop from glycogen and water (often 1–2 kg)
that isn't fat loss; judge progress from the trend after the first
fortnight.

## Limitations

- Anyone on insulin or other glucose-lowering medication must involve
  their clinician before restricting carbohydrate — doses that were
  right before can become dangerous.
- The energy estimate inherits Mifflin–St Jeor's individual error
  (±10% is common); adjust from real-world results after 3–4 weeks.
- Net carbs (total minus fibre) is the convention this tool uses —
  label reading varies by country, since UK/EU labels already exclude
  fibre from "carbohydrate".
