## Methodology

This tool implements the US Navy circumference method exactly as published
by Hodgdon and Beckett (Naval Health Research Center, 1984), working in
centimetres with base-10 logarithms:

  <thead>
    <tr><th scope="col">Sex</th><th scope="col">Equation (% body fat)</th></tr>
  </thead>
  <tbody>
    <tr>
      <td>Men</td>
      <td><code>495 ÷ (1.0324 − 0.19077 × log₁₀(waist − neck) + 0.15456 × log₁₀(height)) − 450</code></td>
    </tr>
    <tr>
      <td>Women</td>
      <td><code>495 ÷ (1.29579 − 0.35004 × log₁₀(waist + hip − neck) + 0.22100 × log₁₀(height)) − 450</code></td>
    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>

The equations estimate body density from the circumference measurements,
then convert to percent fat. In the original validation they correlated
about r = 0.90 with underwater weighing.

**Measuring well matters more than anything else**: use a flexible,
non-stretch tape, snug against the skin without compressing it. Neck: just
below the larynx, tape sloping slightly down to the front. Waist: at the
navel for men; at the narrowest point for women. Hips (women): at the
widest point. Take each measurement two or three times and average.

## Interpreting your result

Every result carries the method's published error band of roughly ±3–4
percentage points, which is why we display a range rather than pretending
to single-decimal accuracy. A reading of 18% really means "most likely
somewhere between about 14.5% and 21.5%".

Where the method shines is **trend tracking**: measurement error is fairly
consistent for a given person, so changes over weeks and months are much
more trustworthy than any single absolute number.

## Limitations

- Circumference methods assume typical fat-distribution patterns; very
  muscular necks, or fat carried atypically, push results off in
  predictable directions.
- Hydration, food timing and tape tension all move readings — measure
  under consistent conditions, ideally first thing in the morning.
- The equations were derived from US Navy personnel and generalise
  reasonably, but not perfectly, to other populations.
- No field method replaces clinical assessment where body composition has
  medical implications.
