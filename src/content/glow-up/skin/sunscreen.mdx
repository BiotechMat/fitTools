## The highest-evidence skincare step, full stop

If a supplement or serum had the evidence sunscreen has, it would be sold out
everywhere. It doesn't get marketed that way because it's cheap and unglamorous,
but the data is unusually good for this field.

### What the trials actually show

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> In the Nambour skin-cancer
trials in Queensland, adults randomised to apply broad-spectrum sunscreen daily
showed roughly **24% less skin ageing** over four and a half years than those
using it at their own discretion, a rare randomised-trial result for a "looks"
outcome.

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> The same trial programme
found **regular sunscreen use reduced melanoma** at long-term follow-up. This is
the health reason that matters most: UV is the main preventable cause of skin
cancer, and the younger-looking skin is a bonus on top.

### How to actually use it

- **Amount.** Most people apply far too little. A rough guide is two
  finger-lengths for face and neck. An under-applied SPF 50 performs like a much
  lower number.
- **When.** Every morning you'll see daylight. Reapply if you're outdoors for a
  long stretch, or after swimming or heavy sweating.
- **Which.** "Broad-spectrum" (UVA + UVB) is the thing to look for. Beyond that,
  the best sunscreen is the one you'll genuinely wear every day, texture and
  feel matter more than brand.

### The claims to be sceptical of

<EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" /> "A base tan protects you", "high SPF is
dangerous", "you don't need it if you're not burning". These keep people out of
the one habit with trial evidence. We take them apart in [sunscreen
myths](/glow-up/skin/sunscreen-myths).

## The honest summary

Daily broad-spectrum sunscreen is the closest thing to a proven glow-up there
is, and it's mainly a health habit, apply enough, every morning, and you've
done the single most effective thing on the list.
