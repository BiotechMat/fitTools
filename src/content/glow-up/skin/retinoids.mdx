## The best-evidenced "anti-ageing" active, honestly

After sun protection, retinoids are the ingredient with the strongest evidence
for improving the look of ageing skin. They're a vitamin-A family of compounds
that nudge skin cells to turn over and prompt collagen production.

### What the evidence supports

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> A systematic review of
randomised controlled trials found topical **tretinoin** consistently improved
the clinical signs of photo-ageing (fine wrinkling, mottled pigmentation,
rough texture) with benefits appearing over months of use. The mechanism is
real: retinoids increase collagen formation and slow its UV-driven breakdown.

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> **Over-the-counter retinol**
is a weaker, gentler cousin. It's converted to the active form in the skin, so
it does less, more slowly, but it's a reasonable lower-irritation starting
point, and better tolerated for beginners.

### How to start without wrecking your skin

- **Low and infrequent first.** A pea-sized amount, two or three nights a week,
  building up as your skin tolerates it. Irritation is the main reason people
  quit, and quitting is what actually costs you results.
- **At night, with sunscreen by day.** Retinoids increase sun sensitivity, so
  they pair with, never replace, your morning sunscreen.
- **Give it months.** This is a slow active. Judge it over 12 weeks, not one.

### The age and pregnancy notes

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> Retinoids **aren't
recommended in pregnancy.** And a note for a younger audience: most under-18s
don't need an anti-ageing retinoid at all. If the goal is treating teenage acne,
that's a conversation with a GP or pharmacist, not a looksmaxxing purchase, and
not the same product decision.

## The honest summary

A retinoid is the one worthwhile "active" to add if ageing is your focus, start
low, be patient, keep wearing sunscreen, and skip it if you're pregnant or
don't actually have a reason for it yet.
