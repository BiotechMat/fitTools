## The trendy actives, sorted by evidence

Every few months a new "hero ingredient" trends. Most are fine, some are
genuinely useful, and none of them out-rank the basics. Here's an honest tier
list.

### Niacinamide

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> One of the better-tolerated
actives, with reasonable evidence for supporting the skin barrier and calming
redness. A sensible, low-risk addition if you want one — just not a
transformation, and not a substitute for sun protection.

### Vitamin C

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> A topical antioxidant with
some evidence for brightening and supporting collagen, but it's finicky —
formulation and stability vary a lot between products, so results are
inconsistent. Reasonable to try; don't expect miracles.

### Hyaluronic acid

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> A humectant that holds water
in the skin, so it makes skin feel plumper and more hydrated in the short term.
Pleasant and low-risk — a comfort ingredient rather than a structural change.

### Collagen (drinks and powders)

<EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" /> The supplement evidence for skin is
preliminary and dominated by industry-funded studies; topical collagen mostly
sits on the surface. Treat it as promising-at-best, not established. Our
[collagen page](/supplements/collagen) has the full breakdown.

### The two that beat all of them

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> Sunscreen and a retinoid.
If you're spending money on skin, spend it there first — the [routine
page](/glow-up/skin/skincare-routine) puts the actives in their place.

## The honest summary

Niacinamide and vitamin C are reasonable extras; hyaluronic acid is a comfort
ingredient; ingestible collagen is oversold. None of them change the ranking:
sun protection and a retinoid do the heavy lifting.
