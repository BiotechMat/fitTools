## What it is

Melanotan II is an unlicensed melanocortin-receptor agonist sold online for
tanning (and sometimes for libido effects). Unlike its regulated cousins in
this section, it has never been approved as a medicine — and it has a
genuinely concerning documented safety record.

## Composition

It is a synthetic cyclic peptide analogue of alpha-melanocyte-stimulating
hormone (alpha-MSH). It is closely related to the approved peptides
afamelanotide (Melanotan I) and bremelanotide (PT-141), which makes the
contrast instructive: same family, very different regulatory and safety
status.

## Proposed mechanism

It activates melanocortin receptors — including MC1R, which stimulates
melanin production (tanning), and MC4R, which influences sexual arousal and
appetite. The tanning effect is real; that is not the issue.

## Claimed benefits

A fast, convenient tan with less sun exposure, and (secondarily) libido
effects. Sellers emphasise the tan and downplay the risks.

<EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" basis="human" />. It is filed here not
because tanning doesn&rsquo;t happen — it does — but because it is marketed as
a safe, convenient shortcut while a documented risk profile and a complete
lack of regulatory oversight are downplayed. Published case reports link
Melanotan II to changes in moles and to melanoma, to prolonged painful
erections (priapism), and to serious systemic reactions including
rhabdomyolysis. Regulators including the UK MHRA have specifically warned
against buying and using it.

## Legal & regulatory status

No Melanotan II product holds a marketing authorisation; it is an unlicensed
medicine, and its sale and supply breach medicines regulations in the UK and
many other countries. It should not be confused with afamelanotide
(Melanotan I / Scenesse), a licensed medicine used under specialist
supervision for a rare light-sensitivity condition.

## Known risks & unknowns

Documented adverse effects include new and changing moles and melanoma
concerns, priapism, nausea and vomiting, flushing, and — in serious cases —
rhabdomyolysis and organ stress. Because it stimulates melanocytes, the
skin-cancer concern is not hypothetical, and there is no monitoring around
unregulated use. This is the compound in this section where the honest
summary is simplest: the risks are real, the product is unlicensed, and
regulators advise against it.
