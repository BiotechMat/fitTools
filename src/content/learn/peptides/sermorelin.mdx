## What it is

Sermorelin is a shortened synthetic version of natural growth-hormone-releasing
hormone (GHRH). Unlike most compounds in this section, it has a genuine medical
history, which the modern "anti-ageing clinic" marketing leans on heavily.

## Composition

It corresponds to the active fragment of GHRH. It has been produced as a
pharmaceutical agent, though the branded product was later discontinued in the
US.

## Proposed mechanism

As a GHRH analogue, it stimulates the pituitary to release the body's own growth
hormone, rather than supplying growth hormone directly.

## Claimed benefits

Reversed ageing, more muscle, better sleep and recovery, the standard
growth-hormone-axis promises.

## What the evidence shows

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />. It can stimulate the body's
own growth-hormone release, which is well documented, and it was historically
used as a test of pituitary function and in assessing growth in children. But the
leap from that to reversing ageing or building muscle in healthy adults is not
supported by good long-term human trials, which is where the modern marketing
overreaches.

## Legal & regulatory status

It has a real medical past but the branded product was discontinued; today's
anti-ageing use sits outside that context. As a GHRH analogue it is on the WADA
Prohibited List and banned in sport at all times.

## Known risks & unknowns

Used medically it was given under supervision; self-directed use for anti-ageing
or physique goals lacks that oversight and the same evidence base.
Research-chemical versions also carry purity and contamination risks.
