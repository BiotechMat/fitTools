## What it is

Epithalon is a synthetic four-amino-acid peptide based on a substance derived
from the pineal gland. It is marketed heavily for longevity and "anti-ageing",
often with claims about lengthening telomeres — the protective caps on
chromosomes.

## Composition

It is a short synthetic peptide (a tetrapeptide). What is sold online is a
laboratory-made research chemical.

## Proposed mechanism

It is claimed to activate telomerase, the enzyme that maintains telomeres, and
thereby slow cellular ageing — the story behind its longevity marketing.

## Claimed benefits

Slowed ageing, longer lifespan and lengthened telomeres.

## What the evidence shows

<EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" basis="mixed" />. The claims rest largely on
animal research and a small number of older human studies, mostly from a single
research group, that fall short of modern standards. There is no robust,
independently replicated human evidence that it slows ageing or meaningfully
lengthens telomeres, so the longevity claims are not supported.

## Legal & regulatory status

Not an approved medicine anywhere, sold as a research chemical. As a non-approved
substance it can fall under WADA's category for substances with no regulatory
approval for human use, so athletes should check current guidance rather than
assume it is permitted.

## Known risks & unknowns

Its long-term safety in humans is essentially unstudied by modern standards, and
research-chemical products carry purity and contamination risks. Any compound
claiming to influence cellular-ageing pathways warrants particular caution given
how little is known.
