## What breathwork is

"Breathwork" covers a range of deliberate breathing techniques, from gentle slow
breathing to intense hyperventilation-and-hold methods. They're promoted for
stress, recovery, focus and more, with a wide gap between the calm, well-founded
end and the extreme, hype-heavy end.

## What the evidence shows

**Slow breathing for acute stress.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Slow, controlled breathing (around six breaths a minute) has reasonable evidence
for calming the nervous system in the moment, lowering stress and nudging
heart-rate variability. It's a genuinely useful, free tool.

**Recovery and sleep wind-down.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Slow breathing before bed or after training aids relaxation, which supports
winding down, a relaxation aid rather than a proven recovery accelerator.

**Intense methods (e.g. Wim Hof).** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Hyperventilation-and-hold protocols have some early research and devoted
advocates, but the evidence for lasting benefits is preliminary, and the safety
cautions matter (see the box).

**Curing disease or dramatic performance claims.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
Sweeping health cures and big performance promises aren't supported.

## How to use it

For everyday stress, a few minutes of slow breathing is enough, and consistency
beats long sessions. **Read the safety box before trying any breath-hold or
hyperventilation method**, never do them in water, while driving, or standing.

## The honest summary

Gentle slow breathing is a genuinely useful, low-risk tool for in-the-moment
stress and winding down. The extreme methods are more experimental, interesting,
but treat the biggest claims, and the safety limits, seriously.
