## Sauna benefits, sorted by evidence

Sauna is one of the few wellness practices where some claims rest on strong
data and others are pure marketing. Here's each, honestly labelled.

### Lower cardiovascular and all-cause mortality

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> Large Finnish
population studies link frequent sauna use with substantially lower
cardiovascular death and lower all-cause mortality, in a dose-response
pattern, more sessions a week, lower risk. This is a genuinely strong
signal. The essential caveat: these are **observational** cohorts, so they
show a strong association, not proof that the sauna itself causes the
benefit. Frequent sauna users may be healthier in other ways.

### Lower blood pressure

<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> Sauna bathing acutely
lowers blood pressure, and regular use is associated with reduced
hypertension risk. Well supported.

### Relaxation and recovery

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> Widely reported and
plausible, with reasonable but less rigorous evidence than the
cardiovascular findings.

### Easing chronic pain and stiffness

<EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" /> Some trials, often with
infrared saunas, suggest benefit for certain chronic-pain and
rheumatological conditions. Promising but not yet definitive.

### "Detoxification" and fat loss

<EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" /> The detox story is marketing; your
liver and kidneys handle that. The scale drop after a session is water you
replace when you rehydrate.

## The honest summary

The strongest reasons to use a sauna are cardiovascular, lower blood
pressure and, in observational data, lower mortality with regular use. Treat
detox and fat-loss claims as marketing. For how often and how long, and the
real safety limits, see the [main guide](/recovery/sauna-therapy).
