## Choosing a home sauna — start with the evidence

Before comparing models, know which type the evidence actually backs. The
strong cardiovascular and mortality data (see the
[main guide](/recovery/sauna-therapy)) comes from **traditional**, hot
Finnish saunas. **Infrared** saunas run cooler and have a smaller, more
condition-specific research base. That distinction should drive the buying
decision more than any feature list.

## Traditional vs infrared

- **Traditional (Finnish).** Hot (around 80°C), with the strongest evidence
  base. Needs more power and better ventilation, and takes longer to heat up.
- **Infrared.** Runs cooler (roughly 45–60°C), heats faster and uses less
  power, and many find it more tolerable — but don't assume the
  traditional-sauna cardiovascular findings transfer to it. Choose it on its
  own merits (comfort, cost, space), not on the Finnish-cohort data.

## The selection criteria that matter

- **Type first** — decide traditional vs infrared based on the point above.
- **Heat-up time and power draw** — traditional units need more; check your
  electrical supply.
- **Size and materials** — enough room to sit comfortably; low-odour,
  suitably rated timber.
- **Ventilation and safety** — proper airflow, a reliable timer and
  temperature cut-off.
- **Where it goes** — indoor vs outdoor changes insulation and weatherproofing
  needs.

## Match the spend to how you'll use it

If you're chasing the cardiovascular evidence specifically, that argues for a
**traditional** sauna used regularly. If you mainly want comfortable,
low-effort heat and relaxation, an **infrared** cabin may suit you better and
cost less to run. Either way, read the safety box above — heat stress,
dehydration and fainting are the real risks, and sauna never mixes with
alcohol.
