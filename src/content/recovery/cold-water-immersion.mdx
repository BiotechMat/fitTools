## What cold water immersion is

Cold water immersion (CWI) — ice baths, cold plunges, cold showers — means
deliberately immersing your body in cold water, typically around 10–15°C,
for a few minutes. It has exploded in popularity, and with it a lot of
overstated claims. This guide sorts what the research actually supports from
what it doesn't.

## What the evidence shows

**Recovery and soreness.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
CWI reliably reduces delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) and the *feeling*
of being recovered, with stronger effects after hard exercise. Whether that
reliably improves next-day performance is less clear.

**The muscle-growth trade-off.** <EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" />
This is the nuance the marketing skips. A 2024 meta-analysis found that CWI
done *immediately* after resistance training tends to **blunt the muscle-
growth response**. If building muscle is a goal, don't ice straight after
lifting — separate them by several hours, or skip it on lifting days.

**Mood and stress.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Some people report feeling better after cold immersion, and a 2025 review
saw improvements in stress — but found no significant change in mood
immediately afterwards, from very limited data. Plausible, not proven.

**Metabolism and cardiovascular adaptation.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Cold is a real physiological stressor and can activate brown fat; some
cardioprotective and metabolic adaptations have been *suggested*. The
long-term human evidence is early.

**Performance enhancement, "boosting immunity", fat loss.**
<EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" /> These are not supported by good
human evidence and are best treated as hype.

## How to start

There's no single evidence-based prescription, and colder or longer is not
automatically better. A sensible starting point is short exposures (a couple
of minutes) in water that feels challenging but tolerable, building up
gradually. If your goal is muscle growth, keep it away from your training;
if it's soreness relief or you simply enjoy it, timing matters less.

Read the safety box above carefully — the cold-shock response is the real
risk, and cold immersion is never something to do alone or after alcohol.
