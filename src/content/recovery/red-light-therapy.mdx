## What red light therapy is

Red light therapy, also called photobiomodulation, exposes skin to specific
wavelengths of red and near-infrared light. It's sold in panels, masks and
handheld devices for recovery, skin and more. It's one of the more scientifically
plausible "light" treatments, which is exactly why the marketing runs ahead of
the evidence.

## What the evidence shows

**Muscle recovery and soreness.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
There's genuine, ongoing research and some positive signals for recovery and
soreness, but results are inconsistent and depend heavily on dose and device.

**Skin.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Among the better-studied uses (wrinkles, some wound healing), with modest effects
in trials.

**Pain and joints.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Some evidence in certain pain conditions; early and mixed.

**Fat loss, whole-body anti-ageing, big performance gains.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
These sweeping consumer claims run well ahead of the evidence.

## A note on devices

Wavelength, power output and distance matter a great deal. An inexpensive panel
may not deliver a dose comparable to the equipment used in studies, so "it worked
in a trial" doesn't guarantee "this gadget will work".

## The honest summary

Red light therapy is a real area of research with preliminary, promising, but
not settled, evidence for recovery and skin. Keep expectations measured, protect
your eyes, and treat the grander claims as marketing.
