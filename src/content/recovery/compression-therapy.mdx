## What compression therapy is

Pneumatic compression boots wrap your legs and inflate in a sequence, squeezing
from the feet upward. The idea is to move blood and fluid to speed recovery.
They've jumped from physiotherapy clinics to athletes' living rooms, along with
some overstated claims.

## What the evidence shows

**Perceived recovery and soreness.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
The most consistent finding is that people *feel* more recovered and report less
muscle soreness after using them. That's a real, if modest, effect.

**Next-day performance.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Whether that translates into measurably better performance the next day is much
less clear, with mixed results.

**Circulation and swelling.** <EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" />
Intermittent pneumatic compression has an established *medical* role (for
conditions like lymphoedema and clot prevention). That's a different use from
athletic recovery. Don't read the medical evidence as proof of a training edge.

**Dramatic performance or "detox" claims.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
Not supported. The boots don't flush toxins or meaningfully boost adaptation.

## How to use them

Typical sessions are 15 to 30 minutes at a comfortable pressure; more isn't better,
and there's no strong evidence for a precise protocol. Read the safety box first. Compression is not for everyone.

## The honest summary

Compression boots are a pleasant, low-effort way to feel more recovered. They're
a convenience tool, not a proven performance booster, sleep, gentle movement and
nutrition do far more for the price of nothing.
