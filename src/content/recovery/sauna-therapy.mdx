## What sauna therapy is

Sauna therapy means regular heat exposure, sitting in a hot room (a
traditional Finnish sauna runs around 80°C) for 15 to 20 minutes. Unusually for
the wellness space, some of its claimed benefits rest on genuinely strong
evidence, so it's worth being precise about which ones.

## What the evidence shows

**Cardiovascular and all-cause mortality.**
<EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" /> Large Finnish cohort
studies (Laukkanen and colleagues) link frequent sauna use to substantially
lower cardiovascular and all-cause mortality, men using a sauna 4 to 7 times a
week had markedly lower risk than once-a-week users, with risk falling as
frequency rose. This is among the best-supported associations in this whole
field. **Important caveat:** it is *observational*, so it shows association,
not proven cause, frequent sauna users may differ in other ways.

**Blood pressure.** <EvidenceTier tier="well-supported" basis="human" />
Randomised and experimental studies show sauna bathing acutely lowers blood
pressure, and regular use is associated with lower hypertension risk.

**Recovery and relaxation.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Reasonable evidence and strong plausibility for relaxation and post-exercise
recovery, though less rigorously quantified than the cardiovascular data.

**"Detox" and weight loss.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" /> The
detox framing is marketing, your liver and kidneys do that job, and
session weight loss is water that returns when you rehydrate.

## Traditional vs infrared

The strong cardiovascular and mortality evidence comes from **traditional**
(hot) Finnish saunas. **Infrared** saunas run cooler and have a smaller,
more condition-specific research base (some trials in heart failure and
chronic pain). Don't assume the traditional-sauna findings transfer to
infrared, a distinction we return to in the
[buying guide](/recovery/sauna-therapy/best-home-saunas).

## How to use it

The cohort benefits were largest at 4 to 7 sessions a week of about 15 to 20
minutes. Hydrate well, cool down gradually, and read the safety box above.
Overheating, dehydration and fainting are the real risks, and sauna and
alcohol don't mix.
