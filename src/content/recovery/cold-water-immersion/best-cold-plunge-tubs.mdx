## How to choose a cold plunge — what actually matters

Cold plunge tubs range from a few tens of pounds (a tub and bags of ice) to
several thousand (a chiller unit with filtration). Before spending, it's
worth being clear on what actually changes the experience — and what you're
paying a premium for.

## The selection criteria that matter

- **Temperature control.** The core question is how reliably it holds your
  target temperature. A powered **chiller** keeps water cold day to day
  without hauling ice; without one, you're topping up with ice each session.
- **Water hygiene.** For repeated use, **filtration and sanitisation**
  matter — otherwise you're changing the water often. This is the main
  practical advantage of the pricier units.
- **Insulation.** Good insulation holds temperature and cuts a chiller's
  running cost.
- **Internal size.** You need to fit and submerge comfortably; check the
  internal dimensions, not the outer footprint.
- **Running cost.** A chiller uses electricity continuously — factor it in.

## Honest alternatives

You do not need an expensive tub to get the cold exposure. A **chest-freezer
conversion** or simply a **stock tank / tub with bags of ice** delivers the
same immersion for a fraction of the price. The expensive units buy
convenience and hygiene for *daily* use — a genuine benefit if you'll plunge
often, and largely wasted money if you won't.

## Match the spend to the habit

If you're not yet sure you'll stick with it, start with the cheapest option
that lets you actually try cold immersion consistently. Upgrade to a chiller
tub only once it's a real habit and the ice-and-water faff is the thing
holding you back. The evidence for cold immersion (see the{" "}
[main guide](/recovery/cold-water-immersion)) doesn't depend on the tub
costing more.
