## What foam rolling is

Foam rolling is a form of self-massage: you use your bodyweight over a foam
cylinder to apply pressure to muscles. It's a gym staple sold on a mix of real,
modest benefits and some persistent myths.

## What the evidence shows

**Short-term range of motion.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Rolling reliably produces a short-term increase in range of motion — and,
usefully, without the temporary strength drop that long static stretching can
cause. That makes it a handy warm-up addition.

**Muscle soreness.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Reasonable evidence that it modestly reduces delayed-onset muscle soreness.

**"Breaking up" fascia or knots.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
The popular idea that rolling physically breaks up fascia or dissolves "knots"
isn't supported. The effects are more likely neural — changing how tight a muscle
*feels* — and short-lived.

**Long-term flexibility and "cellulite".** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
The range-of-motion boost is mostly acute; lasting flexibility comes from regular
mobility and full-range strength work, not rolling alone.

## How to use it

Use it in a warm-up to feel looser, or afterwards for soreness — around a minute
per muscle is plenty. Read the safety box: roll muscle, not joints, spine or
injuries, and keep pressure moderate.

## The honest summary

Foam rolling is a cheap, useful tool for short-term mobility and soreness. Drop
the fascia-and-knots story, keep the realistic expectations, and it earns its
place in a warm-up.
