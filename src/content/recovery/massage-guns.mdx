## What percussive therapy is

A massage gun delivers rapid percussive taps to a muscle. It's a convenient,
self-applied version of massage, marketed for recovery, mobility and performance.
Here's what it actually does.

## What the evidence shows

**Short-term range of motion.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Brief use can increase range of motion in the short term, usefully, without the
temporary strength loss that long static stretching can cause. Good for feeling
looser around a session.

**Muscle soreness.** <EvidenceTier tier="preliminary" basis="human" />
Some evidence that it modestly eases delayed-onset muscle soreness. Helpful for
comfort, not a dramatic effect.

**Performance, strength and muscle growth.** <EvidenceTier tier="marketing-claim" />
No good evidence that percussive therapy boosts strength, speed or growth. Its
job is comfort and short-term mobility, not adaptation.

## How to use one

Short bouts, roughly 1 to 2 minutes per muscle, are plenty, before training for
warm-up mobility or afterwards for soreness. Read the safety box: keep it on
muscle, away from bones, joints, nerves and injuries, and don't grind hard.

## The honest summary

A massage gun is a genuinely handy comfort-and-mobility tool with modest,
short-term benefits. Treat the performance marketing with scepticism and keep
sessions brief.
